## Executive Summary

Devin Reed's **95-5 Content System** reframes B2B SaaS marketing around a structural market reality: at any moment, only ~5% of your total addressable market is actively buying. The other 95% are not looking for a demo — but they *are* looking to learn, form opinions, and decide who feels credible. Most GTM stacks — paid capture, outbound, product-centric LinkedIn — over-invest in fighting over that 5%, while competitors compound recall with the 95% who will enter market months or years later.

Reed built this system at Gong during hypergrowth ($20M → $220M ARR): one year of zero product talk on LinkedIn, educational content mapped to the buyer's daily workflow, and multi-channel distribution (LinkedIn, podcast, webinars, email). Results included 12K → 220K LinkedIn followers, 100K podcast downloads in 18 months, 28% email open rates, and millions in pipeline — with sales reps using content in live calls and buyers arriving at demos pre-trusting the brand.

The system is not a posting tactic. It is an **always-on thought-leadership content engine** with four deployment phases: audit and design (POV, content blueprint, channel playbooks), install infrastructure (roles, tools, tracking), optimize and stress-test for scale, then execute strategic creative bets. Reed now packages this for B2B tech companies through advisory engagements and the Executive LinkedIn Accelerator.

For B2B SaaS marketers, the significance is strategic: LinkedIn organic becomes **competitive infrastructure** — not a lead-gen shortcut. When buyers enter market, they recall two or three brands instantly. If you are not on that consideration list, you have already lost — and CRM last-touch attribution will never show it. The 95-5 system provides the content architecture, executive visibility model, and measurement framework (early vs. lagging indicators) to invest in recall before demand exists.

## Key Themes and Insights

### The 95-5 Rule

Reed's rule aligns with research from the Ehrenberg-Bass Institute (Professor John Dawes) and independently validates what Reed implemented at Gong years earlier: **95% of your addressable market is not in-market; 5% is.**

| Dimension | 5% Marketing (Default) | 95-5 System |
|---|---|---|
| **Audience** | In-market buyers actively evaluating | Out-of-market buyers forming opinions |
| **Content** | Product features, demos, comparisons | Education, POV, workflow value |
| **LinkedIn role** | Company page announcements | Executive thought leadership |
| **CTAs** | Book a demo, talk to sales | Newsletter, guide, follow, save |
| **Measurement** | MQLs, form fills, last-touch | Recall, engagement depth, influenced pipeline |
| **Time horizon** | Quarterly campaign cycles | Multi-year compounding IP |
| **Outcome** | Fight for scraps with competitors | Top of consideration list when buyer enters market |

**Core philosophy:** No one cares about your product until they believe you understand their problem. Reed's Gong rule — enforced for a full year — was explicit: no features, no demos, no "why Gong is better" posts. Product marketing owned the 5%; content marketing owned the 95%.

**The consideration-list mechanism:** When a buyer decides "it's time," they do not start research from scratch. They instantly recall two or three brands. Reed's goal is not just to make the list — but to **lead** it, so competitors are compared against you rather than the reverse.

**Pull vs. push:** Reed's sales background drives the framing. Outbound chases people who did not ask to be contacted. Content creates a pull dynamic — programmatic series (podcasts, newsletters, LinkedIn) that adds value to the 95% so when they enter the 5% window, your brand already leads their consideration set.

**Seven signs you are stuck in 5% marketing** (from The Reeder framework page):

1. Expensive keyword knife-fights
2. More content, flat engagement and leads
3. No flagship content or POV you are known for
4. Executives only show up for launches
5. Every initiative must prove pipeline before it exists
6. Brand deprioritized to the point the board notices
7. Leads-obsessed team with no demand-creation foundation

### Long-Term Demand Creation

Reed distinguishes **activity-based content** (campaigns that reset quarterly) from **memory-based content** (IP that compounds year over year). Buyers reward familiarity, not volume.

**Audience obsession at Gong:** Reed's team mapped the sales rep's journey to quota — pipeline creation, cold emails, discovery calls, demos, pricing conversations — and turned each friction point into standalone educational content. They used insider language ("pipeline" not "pipelines") to signal peer credibility, not vendor positioning.

**Storytelling for retention:** Gong Labs research used screenwriting technique (*in medias res*) — dropping readers into a high-tension scenario before delivering data. Emotional resonance made informational content memorable in ways pure data could not.

**Evergreen + timely balance:** Reed balances posts relevant for six days (industry news, platform changes) with problems and how-tos valid two years ago and two years from now. He asks: *"Is this post going to be relevant in six months, or only six days?"* Safer to over-index on evergreen; timely commentary builds "in the know" reputation when category attention peaks.

**Brand measurement, not just funnel measurement:** At Gong, Reed tracked audience growth (LinkedIn followers, podcast downloads, newsletter subscribers), direct web traffic (URL typed — a recall signal), and inbound opportunities that arrived without outbound chasing. Qualitative signals mattered equally: reps using content in onboarding, leaders sharing posts in Slack, buyers saying *"We've been following you for a while"* on discovery calls.

**The long-game buy-in argument:** When stakeholders ask "Is LinkedIn too saturated?" Reed flips the question: *"When you're looking for new ideas or products, where do you go?"* Answer: network, LinkedIn, newsletters, podcasts — exactly where buyers spend time. Documented strategy (who you help, what problems you solve, where you show up, how you measure) becomes armor against quarterly ROI pressure.

### Executive LinkedIn Programs

Reed treats executive visibility as **GTM infrastructure**, not promotional activity. Invisible executives lose deals before pipeline exists — buyers on LinkedIn choose competitors whose leaders show up consistently.

**Left Door vs. Right Door:** Personal profiles see ~5× the reach of company pages. The company page is a "landing strip" — buyers verify legitimacy after seeing an employee post — but humans earn trust; logos do not. Reed advises against forcing 100% profile-banner compliance; authenticity beats corporate uniformity.

**Five post types framework:** Reed productizes executive LinkedIn into repeatable formats (framework carousels, client stories, contrarian takes, behind-the-scenes, curated commentary) rather than one-off thought leadership. The Executive LinkedIn Accelerator delivers the 95-5 system as full-service strategy + execution for leaders who need credibility fast.

**CEO Slide alignment:** Before launching any content program, Reed maps initiatives to the CEO's quarterly board slide — the 3–5 company-critical initiatives ("Win the Enterprise," "Launch in EMEA," "Dominate the Category"). Example: pitch a podcast not because podcasts are trendy, but because the CEO slide says "Win the C-Suite" and data shows executives listen to podcasts, not whitepapers. If a marketing leader cannot reference the CEO slide, Reed considers them on "Content Island" — building things that might go viral but are not connected to company direction.

**Client case study receipts:**

| Company | Executive LinkedIn outcome |
|---|---|
| Centrical | Executive LinkedIn crossed 20,000 followers; LinkedIn thought-leadership playbook launched |
| myInterview | Clear POV + newsletter + executive LinkedIn; acquired 9 months after 95-5 deployment |
| Vendr | 54% increase in day-of-launch views for quarterly flagship content |

**Anti-viral filter:** Reed publicly turned down a CEO whose goal was to "go viral" and "be famous." Reputation with the right 1,000 people beats reach to 50,000 wrong people. On G2, he stated: 50,000 likes on a puppy photo loses to 50 meaningful comments from CFOs in your ICP — because LinkedIn distributes to similar profiles after peer engagement.

### Content Distribution Systems

Reed's full-stack thinking: **building content is half the job; intentional distribution completes the system.** Organic LinkedIn alone is insufficient for 95% market coverage.

**Multi-channel presence:** Gong showed up where buyers learn differently — LinkedIn posts and blog articles for readers, podcasts for audio-first audiences, webinars and short video for visual learners. "Raving fans" consumed across multiple channels, deepening connection and eliminating format weak spots.

**Channel funnel architecture:** Reed's personal system (documented in his LinkedIn article) follows Plan → Unlock → Produce → Observe. Each channel serves a dedicated function within a larger system — not random acts of marketing. LinkedIn is discovery; newsletter is depth and owned audience; podcast extends reach; advisory/consulting is monetization. People skip around the funnel; the system accommodates non-linear entry.

**Repurpose one asset into 20+ pieces:** A 60-minute webinar becomes clips, posts, email segments, and sales outreach snippets. One flagship piece per quarter — research report, live event, customer study — should fuel weekly LinkedIn for months. Vendr's quarterly flagship content engine is the reference model.

**Newsletter sponsorship playbook:** Reed advises CMOs on paid distribution that still serves the 95%: lead with educational offers (benchmark, guide), not demo CTAs; ask for click-to-open rates, not subscriber counts; know the issue subject line (it filters who sees your ad); open with curiosity — claim, story, or finding — never "Acme is the leading platform for…" He has seen **8× performance differences** from the first two sentences alone.

**Community over broadcast:** Reed shifted from polished broadcast content to behind-the-scenes, audience-involved formats — logo polls, event city votes, CFO clients inviting peers into newsletters. Buyers detect manufactured content; vulnerability and process transparency build deeper relationships than market-friendly polish.

### Building Category Authority

Reed frames content strategy as **category leadership infrastructure** — systematic daily reinforcement of positioning, not quarterly campaign bursts.

**POV as the foundation:** The 95-5 engagement delivers a documented POV the company can repeat, rules for what to publish and what not to, and thought-leadership IP across LinkedIn, email, and events. Content that competes with itself is a symptom of missing POV; content that compounds is a symptom of systematic strategy.

**Differentiation through narrative, not volume:** Reed's closing Attributed advice: stop trying to outdo competitors on volume; outmaneuver their narrative. Find a POV that does not exist. Identify what your audience craves but is not getting. Differentiation wins deals — a sales rep's intuition applied to marketing.

**Flagship content as authority anchors:** Centrical Labs original research, Gong Labs data-backed sales insights, and Vendr's quarterly flagship reports create citation-worthy IP that earns mindshare beyond the feed. Category authority requires assets competitors cannot replicate in a week.

**Content Island as the anti-pattern:** Reed names the siloed content department that churns blog posts reacting to sales requests without strategy — isolated from the revenue engine. Escaping Content Island requires speaking the language of the business (CEO slide), building bridges to revenue (leading/lagging dashboards), and installing systematic infrastructure rather than chasing the latest tactic.

**Five-step startup content strategy** (from LinkedIn carousel, May 2026):

1. Start with the CEO's goals
2. Define your North Star ("What do I want to be known for?")
3. Choose your content type
4. Distribute intentionally
5. Tell stories that sell (transformation narratives, not feature lists)

Audit paralysis — 51-page SEO decks listing 50 fixes — is the enemy. Strategy starts with positioning clarity, not Tuesday's post.

## Key Takeaways

1. **95% of your market is not buying — but they are forming opinions.** Invest in recall before demand exists; capture tactics alone fight over scraps.
2. **One year, zero product talk at Gong** is the canonical proof point: 12K → 220K LinkedIn followers, millions in pipeline, reps weaponizing content in sales calls.
3. **5% marketing asks "how do we capture demand?"** 95-5 asks **"how do we become the obvious choice when demand appears?"**
4. **Buyers reward familiarity, not volume.** Memory-based content compounds; activity-based content resets every quarter.
5. **Executive LinkedIn is competitive infrastructure** — personal profiles ~5× company page reach; invisible leaders lose before CRM shows a loss.
6. **Find the CEO Slide** before pitching any content program — tether initiatives to board-level priorities or stay on Content Island.
7. **Split measurement into early indicators** (views, followers, engagement) **and lagging indicators** (pipeline sourced/influenced, revenue closed). Educational content CTAs should convert to followers and subscribers, not demos.
8. **Resonance beats reach** — 50 ICP comments outperform 50,000 likes from the wrong audience; LinkedIn distributes to similar profiles after peer engagement.
9. **Distribution ≠ creation** — multi-channel playbooks, newsletter sponsorships, and repurposing systems complete the 95-5 engine.
10. **Content systems, not content calendars** — POV documents, channel playbooks, ownership rules, and compounding IP replace quarterly campaign resets.

## Practical Applications for B2B SaaS Marketers

### 1. Run a 95-5 budget audit

Categorize every marketing dollar as **demand capture** (paid search, retargeting, bottom-funnel outbound, G2/review optimization) or **demand creation** (LinkedIn organic, podcast, newsletter, flagship research). If capture dominates, competitors are compounding recall with the 95% while you fight over the 5%.

### 2. Institute a "no product talk" quarter on LinkedIn

Replicate Gong's experiment: 90 days educating your buyer's daily workflow without features, demos, or competitor comparisons. Measure follower growth, saves, rep usage in sales calls, and self-reported "LinkedIn/content" on closed-won deals — not form fills from posts.

### 3. Map content to the CEO Slide

Before any program launch, identify the 3–5 board-level initiatives and pitch content as a business solution: *"We need an executive podcast because the CEO slide says Win the C-Suite, and C-suite buyers consume podcasts, not whitepapers."* This moves content from cost center to strategic partner.

### 4. Build a two-tier measurement dashboard

| Tier | Metrics | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| **Leading** | LinkedIn views, follower growth, engagement, email subscribers, open rates, web traffic (direct + branded search) | Daily pulse; visibility health |
| **Lagging** | Pipeline sourced, pipeline influenced, revenue closed, self-reported attribution | Prove Q1 LinkedIn post influenced Q3 closed-won |

Match CTA intensity to content intent: educational posts → newsletter subscribe; comparison pages → demo request.

### 5. Productize executive LinkedIn into five post types

Do not ask your CEO to "post more." Deploy repeatable formats with weekly cadence: framework carousels, client stories, contrarian takes, behind-the-scenes, curated commentary. Company page verifies; personal profile earns trust.

### 6. Create a "pre-trust signals" collection doc

Screenshots of target-account engagement, rep-forwarded posts, webinar comments from ICP titles, discovery-call quotes (*"I've followed you for a year"*). Lagging indicators will not appear in CRM without deliberate qualitative collection.

### 7. Add newsletter sponsorship as 95% distribution

Identify 3–5 newsletters with proven click-to-open rates in your ICP. Sponsor with educational lead magnets. Apply the 8× hook rule to LinkedIn openers: claim → story → finding, never product description.

### 8. Repurpose one flagship asset per quarter

One research report, live event, or customer study → 20+ LinkedIn posts, email segments, podcast clips, and sales enablement snippets. Weekly feed content should be a derivative of quarterly IP, not net-new every Tuesday.

### 9. Balance evergreen and timely content

Default to problems and how-tos valid two years from now. Jump on timely moments when category attention peaks. Ask before publishing: *six months or six days?*

### 10. Qualify for reputation, not reach

If success metrics are impressions, you will optimize for puppies and platitudes. If metrics are ICP resonance, you will optimize for trust. Define which game you are playing before hiring creators or agencies.

## Relevance to LinkedIn Organic Content Strategy

LinkedIn is the **primary trust engine** in Reed's 95-5 system — not a lead-gen channel, but the daily surface where the 95% encounter your POV, form familiarity, and decide who feels credible before any RFP.

**Strategic role:** LinkedIn organic earns mindshare with out-of-market buyers. Paid LinkedIn captures in-market intent. Most SaaS teams invert this — product announcements on organic, education buried in blog posts nobody finds.

**Executive-led distribution:** Reed centers personal profiles over company pages. For B2B SaaS, the CEO/founder feed is competitive intelligence for buyers. Absence is a silent loss that never hits the P&L directly. Competitors whose leaders educate on LinkedIn create pipeline that does not show as "marketing sourced" until months later.

**Content-sales loop:** Gong posts were not top-of-funnel decoration — reps forwarded them in live conversations, prospects saved them, buyers referenced them before "Book a demo." LinkedIn content becomes sales enablement when it educates the buyer's workflow instead of pitching product.

**CTA architecture for LinkedIn:** Soft conversions dominate 95% content — newsletter signup, follow, save, guide download. Demo CTAs are reserved for the 5% window. Jamming "Book a demo" into educational posts creates the trap Reed sees in content audits: high views from people who figured out you exist 30 seconds ago, zero pipeline because they are not ready.

**Measurement on LinkedIn:** Track views and follower growth as early indicators. Pair with self-reported attribution ("How did you hear about us?") and influenced pipeline for lagging proof. Finance pushback is predictable — Reed's framework anticipates the "show me ROI in 30 days" objection with qualitative ICP engagement screenshots.

**Format preferences:** Reed packages strategy in carousel/document posts (numbered frameworks, case studies). Comment threads on his posts surface practitioner debate (finance attribution, solo content hire access) — validating frameworks land with operators, not just marketers.

## Common Patterns Observed

### Client story → framework → soft CTA

Reed rarely posts abstract advice. He opens with a named client scenario ("A CMO I advise…," "My client was drowning in a 51-page SEO audit…"), extracts a numbered framework, and closes with a soft CTA — swipe the carousel, register for Live, subscribe to newsletter. Every major claim ties to Gong/Clari tenure: 12K → 220K followers, 28% email opens, 8× hook performance, $20M → $200M ARR. Reed sells systems because he built them at scale.

### Sales accountability applied to marketing

Binary sales mindset — you hit quota or you don't — applied to content: either growing the business or costing the business. Rejects "we're doing other things" as a marketing excuse.

### Anti-viral positioning as brand filter

Public rejection of vanity-metric clients attracts the right advisory engagements. Niche virality (1,000 right people) beats mass reach (50,000 wrong people).

### One thesis, many formats

95-5 doctrine appears in newsletter editions, carousels, podcast appearances, G2 interviews, and client case studies. Category ownership through repetition — a pattern Chris Walker and Adam Robinson also practice.

### Educational offers at every funnel stage

Organic LinkedIn, newsletter sponsorship, webinar registration — all default to guides, benchmarks, and how-tos. Demo CTAs reserved for the 5% window.

### Content systems language over calendar language

Reed consistently frames marketing as **infrastructure** — POV documents, channel playbooks, ownership rules, compounding IP — not quarterly campaigns that reset.

Chris Walker's 95–99% not-in-market framing and Reed's 95-5 are conceptually aligned; Walker adds organizational design and attribution infrastructure where Reed adds content operating systems and executive deployment tools.

### Most actionable lessons for playbook development

1. **Lead every program with the 95-5 budget audit and CEO Slide alignment** — without these, tactical posting produces Content Island noise.
2. **Deploy the Gong "no product talk" experiment as a 90-day pilot** with pre-defined early/lagging indicators and a qualitative "pre-trust signals" doc.
3. **Institute soft CTA architecture on LinkedIn** — newsletter and follow for educational posts; demo only on high-intent assets.
4. **Productize executive LinkedIn** into five repeatable post types with Left Door (personal profile) as primary broadcast and company page as verification landing strip.
5. **Build the measurement stack before scaling content** — Walker's self-reported attribution + Reed's leading/lagging dashboard prevents finance from killing the program at month 3.
6. **One flagship asset per quarter → 20+ derivatives** — systematic repurposing replaces the content calendar hamster wheel.
7. **Pair Reed's strategic purpose with van der Blom's distribution SOPs and Acosta's email funnel** — no single expert covers the full stack; the playbook should integrate all three layers.
