## Summary

Richard describes LinkedIn in 2026 as **"turbulent"**: a fundamental algorithm restructure at the end of 2025, a flood of AI-generated content and outreach, third-party analytics tools being shut down (Shield, UseAware), and a measurable exodus of users who no longer recognize the platform.

The core thesis of the episode is that LinkedIn has completed its shift from a **relationship graph** (distribute to your network) to an **interest graph** (distribute by topical relevance). LinkedIn's stated goal — "relevant reach, not virality" — shows up in the data: active creators have lost **~60% of reach over two years**, while engagement is down only **20–25%**, meaning fewer views but more engagement per view.

Richard walks through his practical response framework:

1. **Topic fingerprinting** — align profile, posts, and comments so LinkedIn understands one expertise domain.
2. **Intentional commenting** — 25–30 minutes daily, split between post-publish nurturing and curated feeds of on-topic peers.
3. **Format and cadence discipline** — 2–4 posts per week (down from 5–6); prioritize carousels and text+image over text-only unless you are an elite copywriter.
4. **LinkedIn newsletters** — dramatically outperform standalone articles on reach, engagement, and conversion; Richard attributes ~80% of his LinkedIn-driven revenue to newsletters vs. feed posts.

Secondary themes include the AI comment crisis (80% of Richard's first-five-minute comments are AI-generated), pod detection (~97% accuracy), suggested posts as "free ads" (9% of feed), and Richard's prediction that LinkedIn is at a crossroads — success depends on whether the platform can purge spam and automation without losing its audience.

## Key Lessons

### 1. The Interest Graph is the biggest shift in a decade

LinkedIn no longer distributes primarily to first-degree connections. **9% of the feed is now "suggested posts"** — content shown to non-followers based on interest-cluster alignment. Reach taken from hyper-active creators is redirected toward casual posters (+10–15%), ads, and suggested distribution. Earning a suggested placement is equivalent to a free ad, but only if post-publish engagement proves the content is valuable to that cluster.

### 2. Topic fingerprinting is the prerequisite for everything else

Three inputs train the algorithm who you are:

| Input | Action |
|---|---|
| **Profile** | Rewrite headline, About, and experience to match current ICP — delete resume-era content that confuses semantic classification. Richard saw measurable reach uplift within three weeks of his February 2026 profile overhaul. |
| **Posts** | Copy last 20 posts into an AI tool. Ask: "What are my two main topics?" Wrong answer = out of lane. Four or five topics = too scattered. Target **one main topic + 2–3 related subtopics**, with ~80% of content staying in lane. |
| **Comments** | Comments now reshape your interest graph, not just human visibility. Off-topic comments (even social courtesy for former clients) dilute your cluster. LinkedIn distinguishes social comments from interest-cluster comments. |

### 3. Commenting off-topic actively hurts reach

Engaging in pods or leaving comments across unrelated topics confuses LinkedIn's model of who you are. Richard stopped commenting on former clients' job posts because it pulls him out of his cluster. Pod behavior is ~97% detectable and aligns with LinkedIn's stated war on manipulative/automated engagement.

### 4. Post-publish nurturing is non-negotiable in 2026

Richard only publishes when he can dedicate **10–15 minutes immediately after**. The first hour determines whether a post earns suggested distribution. Internal comments (replying on your own post) and external comments (on others' on-topic content) are equally important — but external comments without strategy deliver "crickets."

**Daily commenting system (~25–30 min):**

| Block | Duration | Activity |
|---|---|---|
| Post-publish | 10–15 min | Respond to early engagement; engage 5–10 on-topic peers from bookmark-search feeds |
| Midday check-in | ~15 min | Client social comments (when relevant); 5–10 comments on second/third-degree discussions in your niche |
| End of day | Remainder | Catch up on bookmark lists not yet reviewed |

### 5. Bookmark-search feeds replace shut-down third-party tools

Create curated engagement lists using LinkedIn's native post search: filter by author names + frequency (daily/weekly), bookmark the URL. Richard maintains separate feeds for top creators (~30 names), clients, prospects, and competitors. This is the free, compliant alternative to tools like Shield and UseAware that LinkedIn has shut down.

### 6. LinkedIn newsletters outperform everything else for conversion

Standalone articles perform at **~0.4× normal reach**. Newsletter editions of long-form content perform "way up" on reach, engagement, and conversion. Richard publishes a weekly Sunday newsletter and attributes **~80% of LinkedIn-driven revenue** (report sales, consulting, community) to newsletters vs. feed posts — despite posting 5× more often in the feed. New analytics now include per-link click tracking. New followers convert to newsletter subscribers at ~60% on Richard's profile (~130K subs).

### 7. Posting frequency has dropped; quality bar has risen

Optimal cadence for the average creator: **2–4 posts per week** (down from 5–6). Daily posting with low-quality filler (memes, quotes) works against you. Richard still posts 6×/week because he has enough insight density — but recommends most creators aim for 2–3.

**If posting 3×/week, Richard's format mix:**

- Text + image
- Carousel (document)
- Text + image
- Plus: newsletter at least monthly (ideally weekly)

Avoid: polls, video (unless you enjoy it), text-only (unless you are an elite copywriter).

### 8. Text-only and video require exceptional craft or they harm authority

Text-only reach is up overall — but only for the **top 5% of copywriters**. For everyone else, numbers are down. Video is "vulnerable": if you don't enjoy speaking on camera, it shows and damages authority. Don't do video because marketing said so.

### 9. Top creators are not a replicable playbook for B2B SaaS

Creators selling LinkedIn/marketing education operate in a bubble where nearly everyone on the platform is a potential buyer. **99.99% of LinkedIn users are not looking to buy SaaS software.** Watch top creators for dynamics (nurturing, topic consistency, format), but translate tactics into your ICP and buying cycle — don't copy cadence or hooks verbatim.

### 10. AI comments are a platform-wide crisis

Richard receives **20–30 comments in the first five minutes** of a post; **80% are AI-generated**, often from the same people. These comments provide no distribution value. Richard now blocks more users than ever. SaaS teams should treat AI comment engagement as noise, not a signal to reciprocate.

## Relevance to LinkedIn Organic Content Strategy for B2B SaaS

### Strategic foundation

This episode is the operational companion to the 2026 Algorithm Insights Report. Where the report provides benchmarks (format multipliers, engagement weights, reach declines), the podcast provides **assignable workflows** for SaaS marketing teams: profile audits, commenting SOPs, bookmark-search feed setup, newsletter launch criteria, and post-publish nurturing windows.

### Direct applications for B2B SaaS

| Insight | SaaS application |
|---|---|
| Interest Graph shift | Narrow founder/executive positioning to 1–3 product-adjacent pillars (e.g., "pipeline analytics for mid-market SaaS") instead of generalist leadership content |
| Topic fingerprinting | Audit CEO and AE profiles with the "last 20 posts" AI test before scaling employee advocacy |
| 60% reach decline | Set stakeholder expectations: impressions will drop; optimize for saves, substantive comments, and profile visits from target accounts |
| Post-publish nurturing | Assign a 60-minute post-publish SOP to the post author or community manager — non-negotiable for executive-led programs |
| Bookmark-search feeds | Build zero-cost ICP engagement lists: prospects, analysts, category peers, and competitors — replaces shut-down automation tools |
| Newsletters | Repurpose existing product updates, changelog digests, or email newsletters as LinkedIn newsletter editions; track per-link clicks for pipeline attribution |
| 2–4 posts/week cadence | Reduce posting volume for most team members; invest saved time in carousel production and comment engagement |
| AI comment crisis | Do not reward AI comments with replies; block repeat offenders; measure comment quality (15+ words from ICP accounts) |
| Suggested posts | Design content for cluster-relevant engagement from second/third-degree connections — this is how SaaS founders reach buyers outside their network |
| Creator playbook caveat | Do not copy LinkedIn-guru cadence (5–6×/week, income screenshots); SaaS buyers need product-adjacent expertise, not creator-economy hooks |

### What to implement first

1. **Profile + topic audit** for every person in the organic program (founder, executives, AEs).
2. **Bookmark-search feeds** for daily ICP engagement (30 min/day across the team).
3. **Post-publish SOP** tied to the first 60 minutes after every post.
4. **Newsletter pilot** — one edition per month minimum, repurposed from existing email or product content.
5. **Format shift** — prioritize document carousels (case studies, workflow comparisons, GTM frameworks) over text-only and video.

### Risk factors for SaaS teams

- **Tool dependency:** Third-party analytics and engagement tools are being shut down; build platform-native workflows now.
- **Employee advocacy without topic discipline:** Having 10 employees comment across random topics dilutes everyone's interest graph.
- **Automation backlash:** LinkedIn is banning accounts at higher rates; any non-compliant outreach tool is a brand risk.
- **Audience exodus:** If spam drives users off the platform, the addressable B2B audience shrinks — quality over volume matters for the ecosystem, not just your metrics.
